# Advanced Strategy — Distilled Knowledge

## Core Philosophy
Advanced strategy in this course is the discipline of creating a real, defendable competitive advantage in the marketplace, not just inventing something interesting or running a better process. Kevin Coyne's lens is brutally simple: the winner has the widest wedge between willingness to pay and cost to serve, and that wedge only matters if it is protected by value, a binding constraint, and an asymmetry. Good strategy is therefore adversarial, quantitative, and structural: it asks what customers value, what stops everyone else from offering it, why you can do it and others cannot, and whether that advantage has power, range, and sustainability.

## Key Frameworks & Models
### Wedge Analysis (Competitive Advantage)
- **What it is**: The core definition of competitive advantage as a wider gap between willingness to pay and cost to serve than rivals have.
- **When to use it**: Any time you need to compare competitors, assess whether a business really has an advantage, or avoid confusing low cost, high price, or innovation with true strategic strength.
- **How it works**:
  1. Estimate the customer's willingness to pay and the firm's cost to serve.
  2. Calculate the wedge: `WTP - CTS`.
  3. Compare wedge width across competitors rather than comparing price, gross margin, or absolute cost in isolation.
  4. Identify whether the wedge comes from higher WTP, lower CTS, or both.
  5. Ask what lets the firm keep that wedge from being competed away.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Who actually has the competitive advantage?
  - Is the edge driven by differentiation, cost, or both?
  - Are we mistaking low cost, high price, or innovation for real advantage?

### Three-Leg Competitive Advantage Test
- **What it is**: Coyne's test that an advantage exists only when value, a binding constraint, and an asymmetry all hold simultaneously.
- **When to use it**: To validate any claimed moat, differentiation, or market position.
- **How it works**:
  1. Confirm that customers genuinely value the offering.
  2. Identify the binding constraint that prevents society from having unlimited supply cheaply from many sellers.
  3. Identify the asymmetry that lets this firm overcome or exploit that constraint better than rivals.
  4. Test whether any of the three legs is missing, weakening, or shifting.
  5. Reject the advantage claim if even one leg fails.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is there real competitive advantage here at all?
  - Which leg is carrying the moat?
  - Which leg is most likely to break first?

### Antes vs. Drivers
- **What it is**: A way to separate table stakes from the attributes that actually drive customer choice.
- **When to use it**: When diagnosing customer value, segmentation, differentiation, or why a once-strong feature no longer matters.
- **How it works**:
  1. List the product or service attributes customers expect.
  2. Separate attributes that must be "good enough" to be considered from attributes that actually influence choice.
  3. Treat the first set as antes and the second set as drivers.
  4. Check whether former drivers have become commoditized and therefore turned into antes.
  5. Identify the next driver before competitors do.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What is merely required to play?
  - What actually moves customer choice?
  - Which differentiators are about to become table stakes?

### Binding Constraint Diagnosis
- **What it is**: A framework for identifying why society cannot have as much of something as it wants, cheaply, from many suppliers.
- **When to use it**: Whenever a market seems to support high margins, concentration, lock-in, scarcity pricing, or natural monopoly behavior.
- **How it works**:
  1. Determine whether the constraint is active in the marketplace now, not merely potential.
  2. Diagnose whether it is supply-side, demand-side, or market-structure based.
  3. For supply-side constraints, test scarcity, patents, know-how, regulation, or limited capacity.
  4. For demand-side constraints, test network effects, switching costs, and customer inertia.
  5. For market-structure constraints, test whether the market only economically supports one or a few players.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Why can't everyone offer this value?
  - Is the moat caused by supply scarcity, demand lock-in, or structure?
  - Is the constraint real, weakening, or about to disappear?

### Five Types of Asymmetry
- **What it is**: Coyne's classification of the strategically relevant differences that matter only if they hit the binding constraint.
- **When to use it**: To explain why one specific firm can win where others cannot.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify the binding constraint first.
  2. Check for structural asymmetry, access to resources or skills, asymmetry of objectives, asymmetry of information, and creative or behavioral asymmetry.
  3. Ignore differences that do not directly affect the constraint.
  4. Evaluate whether the asymmetry is durable or easily copied.
  5. Match the asymmetry type to the way the firm plans to compete.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Why this firm and not another?
  - What kind of asymmetry is doing the work?
  - Is the firm's claimed difference strategically relevant or just cosmetic?

### Fixed vs. Variable Cost Decision Framework
- **What it is**: A decision-based method for classifying costs based on the action being considered and the timeframe, rather than on accounting labels.
- **When to use it**: In pricing, retaliation, capacity, operating leverage, and competitor-response analysis.
- **How it works**:
  1. Define the exact decision on the table.
  2. Define the timeframe of that decision.
  3. Test each cost item by asking whether it can actually be avoided within that timeframe.
  4. Classify avoidable costs as variable and unavoidable costs as fixed.
  5. Re-run the classification if the decision context changes.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Which costs really move if volume changes?
  - What is the true contribution margin in this decision?
  - Are we about to make a pricing decision using the wrong cost logic?

### Cost-Structure Weapon Framework (HF/LV vs. LF/HV)
- **What it is**: A framework for understanding how high-fixed/low-variable and low-fixed/high-variable businesses can attack one another differently.
- **When to use it**: In price wars, retaliation decisions, market-entry defense, or product-line battles.
- **How it works**:
  1. Determine whether each player is primarily high fixed/low variable or low fixed/high variable.
  2. If you are HF/LV, evaluate price cuts, pricing above your own variable cost but below theirs, fixed-cost escalation, and innovation-driven share grabs.
  3. If you are LF/HV, evaluate product proliferation and forcing product or process changes that make the HF/LV player retool.
  4. Calculate kill price, withdrawal price, and the volume needed after any price cut.
  5. Adjust for price sensitivity before using price as a weapon.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Which side can survive aggressive pricing?
  - Which attack weapon fits this cost structure?
  - When is price cutting brilliant and when is it suicidal?

### Four-Step Competitor Cost Estimation
- **What it is**: A practical process for estimating a rival's cost position from partial information.
- **When to use it**: When judging whether a challenger can really make money, or whether an incumbent can credibly retaliate.
- **How it works**:
  1. Remove structural costs that are roughly equal across competitors.
  2. Adjust for efficiency differences using credible benchmarks.
  3. Adjust for capacity utilization and load factors.
  4. Adjust for factor-cost differences only after productivity and utilization are addressed.
  5. Sanity-check the result against known profitability and market behavior.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Can the entrant actually survive at that price?
  - How much better is the rival's cost position really?
  - Which source of advantage matters most: structure, efficiency, utilization, or input cost?

### Six Routes to Low Cost
- **What it is**: Coyne's six distinct ways to achieve lower cost to serve.
- **When to use it**: When designing or diagnosing a cost advantage instead of defaulting to layoffs or generic efficiency talk.
- **How it works**:
  1. Test whether the edge comes from leapfrog technology, tailoring the value chain, control of the lowest-cost input, factor-cost arbitrage, economies of scale, or economies of scope.
  2. Specify which route is primary and which reinforcements make it hard to copy.
  3. Quantify how the route changes CTS, not just one input cost.
  4. Check whether the route survives competitor response.
  5. Avoid claiming "low cost" without a clear route.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Where does the cost edge actually come from?
  - Is the route structural or easily copied?
  - Which route best fits this business model?

### Nine Vulnerabilities of Low-Cost Strategy
- **What it is**: A stress test for low-cost positions that often look stronger on paper than they are in practice.
- **When to use it**: Before scaling, investing in fixed assets, consolidating, offshoring, or launching a low-cost attack.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify which low-cost route the firm is relying on.
  2. Check whether scale really materializes automatically and whether the market will actually respond.
  3. Test for technology lock-in, quality problems from distance, and the endless "race to forever" of cost pressure.
  4. Test for union risk, customization pressure, powerful customers, and market-share wars.
  5. Reject or redesign the strategy if one of these vulnerabilities dominates the economics.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What could break this low-cost strategy?
  - Is the business becoming efficient or merely rigid?
  - Who will capture the value from our lower cost: us, customers, or powerful channels?

### Learning Curves and the BCG Logic
- **What it is**: The idea that cumulative production drives down unit cost, which underpins the strategic logic behind Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, and Dogs.
- **When to use it**: In markets where cumulative experience improves yield, quality, or cost, and where share today affects cost tomorrow.
- **How it works**:
  1. Estimate the learning rate and how cost declines with each doubling of cumulative volume.
  2. Compare leaders and followers on cumulative, not just current, output.
  3. Ask whether the lagging player can realistically catch the leader's learning position.
  4. If yes, treat it as a Question Mark and invest hard; if no, avoid wishful thinking.
  5. Reinforce the learning curve with organizational choices that speed discovery and dissemination.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Does market share today create tomorrow's cost advantage?
  - Can the follower catch up, or should it exit?
  - How do organizational design choices accelerate learning?

### Strategy Definition Framework
- **What it is**: Coyne's definition of strategy as the short list of decisions that are hard to reverse and largely dictate everything else.
- **When to use it**: When turning analysis into an actual strategic position rather than a collection of tactics.
- **How it works**:
  1. Decide where to compete across product range, customer segment, geography, vertical scope, business model, and unique competence.
  2. Decide how to compete by specifying the source of wedge expansion.
  3. Design the value chain so operating choices reinforce the position.
  4. Check that the three pieces are mutually reinforcing.
  5. Set a planning horizon that matches how fast the industry changes.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What decisions are truly strategic here?
  - Where exactly will we play?
  - Do our operating choices reinforce the claimed advantage?

### Six Big-Beats-Small Weapons
- **What it is**: The six classic ways large players can use size as a strategic weapon.
- **When to use it**: When assessing incumbent power, scale threats, consolidation, or why a small player is getting squeezed.
- **How it works**:
  1. Test for economies of scale, fixed-cost escalation, learning curves, economies of density, scope and bundling, and deep pockets.
  2. Identify which one the incumbent can actually deploy in this market.
  3. Quantify whether the smaller rival must match dollar-for-dollar or can respond proportionally.
  4. Check whether the attack is local, global, or density-based.
  5. Ask which weapon is most dangerous given the rival's structure.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - How can the large player crush or discipline smaller rivals?
  - Which size-based weapon is relevant here?
  - Can the smaller player neutralize the incumbent's size advantage?

### Six Small-Beats-Big Strategies
- **What it is**: Coyne's six ways smaller firms can beat larger incumbents despite scale disadvantages.
- **When to use it**: In startup strategy, challenger positioning, and anti-incumbent go-to-market design.
- **How it works**:
  1. Test whether the right move is indirect attack, judo, exploiting homogeneity requirements, cherry-picking, spoiler strategy, or race-and-sell.
  2. Choose the route that makes the incumbent's size a liability rather than a strength.
  3. Keep the target narrow enough that the incumbent's rational response is delayed, too expensive, or structurally awkward.
  4. Build quickly within that protected pocket.
  5. Expand only after the asymmetry is secured.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - How does a startup survive against a much bigger incumbent?
  - Where is the incumbent over-averaging, over-defending, or overcommitted?
  - How can a challenger make the incumbent's response uneconomic?

### Power Analysis
- **What it is**: A framework for assessing whether an existing advantage can actually be converted into share shifts or industry-structure changes.
- **When to use it**: After confirming an advantage exists and before assuming the advantaged firm can dominate the market.
- **How it works**:
  1. Check for desire, means, and resources.
  2. Calculate the additional wedge versus the competitor.
  3. Estimate customer acquisition cost.
  4. Compute the power ratio and compare it to the rough 1.5x to 2.0x threshold.
  5. Adjust for customer fluidity, deep pockets, learning effects, increasing returns, and sustainability.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Can this firm turn advantage into market share gains?
  - Is the advantage merely profitable, or actually powerful?
  - What blocks the firm from acting on its edge?

### Range Analysis
- **What it is**: A framework for defining the boundaries within which an advantage actually holds.
- **When to use it**: Before expansion into new products, customer segments, geographies, or adjacent capabilities.
- **How it works**:
  1. Test whether value transfers to the new domain.
  2. Test whether the binding constraint or cost dynamics still apply there.
  3. Test whether the same asymmetry still exists against those specific competitors.
  4. Check product, customer, geographic, and core-knowledge range.
  5. Distinguish between binary limits and graduated limits.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Does our moat travel?
  - Where does our advantage weaken or disappear?
  - Are we about to overextend outside our real domain of strength?

### Sustainability Analysis
- **What it is**: A way to judge how long an advantage will last by tracking the durability of value, the binding constraint, and the asymmetry.
- **When to use it**: When choosing between strategies, funding a business, or deciding whether to trigger an aggressive attack.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify which of the three legs is most likely to erode first.
  2. Examine whether the erosion is due to value shifts, binding-constraint release, or asymmetry catch-up.
  3. Look for gradual weakening rather than waiting for binary collapse.
  4. For startups, evaluate sustainability at steady-state economics rather than launch-stage costs.
  5. Prefer strategies whose advantages are more durable, not just more exciting.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - How long is this edge likely to last?
  - What will kill it first?
  - Is now the time to exploit it aggressively or preserve optionality?

### Patent Strategy for Entrepreneurs
- **What it is**: A practical framework for using patents as strategic tools rather than treating them as generic badges of innovation.
- **When to use it**: When an idea depends on proprietary technology, scientific discovery, or defensibility against imitation.
- **How it works**:
  1. Decide whether the protectable asset is composition, use, or method, with strong preference for composition or use.
  2. File before any public disclosure and treat prior art as globally fatal.
  3. Draft broad claims first, typically with "comprising" language, knowing claims only get narrowed later.
  4. Map freedom to operate and possible mutual-blocking scenarios.
  5. Judge whether the patent is actually enforceable given geography, litigation cost, and the firm's ability to sue.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Should this company patent, and what kind of patent matters most?
  - Does the patent create a real binding constraint or just paperwork?
  - Is the firm protected, blocked, or both?

### Venture Capital Bargaining Framework
- **What it is**: A framework for understanding how VCs price risk, structure control, and shift value from founders to investors.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating whether a venture-backed business is fundable and whether financing terms destroy strategic freedom.
- **How it works**:
  1. Start from VC economics: most bets fail, so winners must overpay for losers.
  2. Expect the 40% post-money gravitational pull and pressure toward future control.
  3. Stress-test process tricks, valuation tricks, and deal-structure tricks such as lockups, ratchets, class rights, and participating preferred.
  4. Preserve time, alternatives, expert counsel, and access to closing documents before signing.
  5. Evaluate financing not just as capital, but as a strategic relationship that changes power.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this financing aligned or predatory?
  - How much strategic freedom is the founder giving away?
  - Will the cap table help or cripple the business after things go wrong?

## Mental Models & Principles
- The marketplace, not the lab, decides whether an advantage is real.
- The widest wedge wins; lowest cost alone does not.
- Value is customer perception, not producer effort.
- Innovation is worthless if a better-positioned incumbent can copy, outspend, or distribute it faster.
- Always ask how revenues and costs change for you and for the adversary.
- Work in actual dollars and units, not abstract percentages that hide asymmetries.
- Ask "variable with what?" and "over what timeframe?" before trusting any cost classification.
- Minimum intervention often beats all-out retaliation.
- Customer inertia can be more powerful than patents.
- First-mover advantage is weak unless converted into structural lock-in.
- Incumbents are often slow, not stupid; exploit the lag created by lock-in, assets, incentives, and habit.
- Range is a silent killer; success in one domain says little about the next.
- Strategy is a short list of hard-to-reverse decisions, not an aspiration statement.
- AI is not a strategy unless the firm has a data, training-set, or workflow advantage others cannot easily match.
- VCs are counterparties with their own economics, not neutral supporters of the founder's dream.

## Common Mistakes & Anti-Patterns
- Confusing low cost with competitive advantage instead of calculating the wedge.
- Treating discovery or invention as market advantage before the business is actually selling.
- Confusing factor-cost advantages with total cost per unit.
- Pricing aggressively in price-insensitive markets and destroying your own margin.
- Calling something a binding constraint when it only affects one player; that is asymmetry, not a market-wide constraint.
- Listing random company differences as asymmetries without showing how they hit the constraint.
- Assuming scale economies happen automatically after growth or acquisition.
- Ignoring how fixed/variable cost classifications change with the decision context.
- Expanding into adjacencies without re-testing value, binding constraint, and asymmetry in the new domain.
- Believing first-mover advantage or patents are self-enforcing.
- Overestimating the transferability of brand, expertise, or technology across products or geographies.
- Letting VCs trap the company on time, lockup, vague pricing, or hidden terms.
- Claiming "AI will solve it" without a credible privileged-data reason.

## How This Discipline Challenges Others
This strategy discipline pushes back on design thinking by saying that user love without a defendable wedge is just a nice prototype. It pushes back on product management by arguing that roadmap quality does not matter if the company cannot capture or defend the value it creates. It pushes back on entrepreneurship by distinguishing validation from value capture: a customer willing to pay is necessary, but not sufficient, if incumbents, channels, or investors will extract the profit. It pushes founders to think adversarially: where is the wedge, what binds supply or demand, who can retaliate, how quickly can others copy, and what part of the value chain actually lets the company keep the money.

## Key Vocabulary & Concepts
- **Wedge**: The gap between willingness to pay and cost to serve.
- **WTP**: Willingness to pay; not the same thing as price.
- **CTS**: Cost to serve; depends on the decision timeframe.
- **Ante**: Table stakes needed to be considered.
- **Driver**: Attribute that actually drives customer choice.
- **Binding constraint**: Reason society cannot have unlimited cheap supply from many sellers.
- **Structural asymmetry**: Hard-to-replicate position or asset.
- **Access asymmetry**: Superior access to resources, skills, or talent.
- **Objectives asymmetry**: Competitive opening created by different goals.
- **Information asymmetry**: Data or insight others lack or misuse.
- **Creative/behavioral asymmetry**: Novel move or behavior others would not pursue.
- **Cherry-picking**: Serving only the profitable slices of an incumbent's business.
- **Judo strategy**: Using the incumbent's size or installed base against it.
- **Spoiler strategy**: Attacking the small subset that creates disproportionate value.
- **Race and sell**: Making a market so painful that a rational entrant leaves.
- **Economies of density**: Cost advantage from geographically or operationally concentrated demand.
- **Learning curve**: Cost decline as cumulative production doubles.
- **Fixed-cost escalation**: Forcing smaller rivals to match large fixed investments.
- **Power**: Ability to convert advantage into share shifts or structural change.
- **Range**: Domain within which the advantage holds.
- **Sustainability**: How long value, the constraint, and the asymmetry remain intact.
- **Kill price**: Price at or below a competitor's variable cost.
- **Withdrawal price**: Price at which a rival survives now but cannot fund asset renewal.
- **Freedom to operate**: Legal assessment of whether you infringe others' patents.
- **Participating preferred**: VC security that lets investors double dip.
- **40% gravitational pull**: The tendency of VC rounds to target roughly 40% ownership.

## Decision Criteria for Business Ideas
- A good idea has a clear wedge versus the realistic alternative, not just a cool product.
- The value proposition must be specific enough that a narrow segment cares intensely, not vaguely.
- The idea must identify a real binding constraint or cost dynamic; if everyone can do it cheaply, strategy is absent.
- The team must show a credible asymmetry that hits that constraint directly.
- The business should survive the question, "What happens when a smarter, richer incumbent notices?"
- The company should know whether its edge comes from WTP, CTS, or both, and how that edge compounds.
- The starting market should sit within the firm's true range; expansion logic must be earned, not assumed.
- The edge should have measurable power, not just profitability on paper.
- The advantage should be reasonably sustainable at steady-state economics.
- Financing strategy matters: if the idea requires VC, the founder must understand whether the cap table will destroy future value capture.
- Bad ideas in this lens are invention-led, moat-free, range-confused, price-undisciplined, and dependent on magical execution or generic AI claims.
