# Silicon Valley Insights — Distilled Knowledge

## Core Philosophy

Silicon Valley's practitioner view is simple: ideas are cheap, distribution is hard, fundraising is a power game, and scale punishes sloppy thinking. Real companies are built by founders who can survive long enough to learn, sell before they automate, choose a narrow beachhead, and compound small advantages into something defensible. The recurring message from founders, operators, and investors across this study tour was that startup success is rarely about elegant theory — it is about customer truth, timing, founder-market fit, speed, distribution, capital efficiency, and the ability to keep going through long periods of ambiguity.

Across all 17 speakers, a meta-pattern emerges: the practitioners who built lasting companies were those who matched missionary conviction with operational realism. Craig Newmark declined billions and ran $700M revenue with 50 people. Mike Kuczynski spent 25 years iterating on a conservation business model. Danny King took no salary for six years. Steve Blank reframed entrepreneurship as a calling, not a career. The common thread is not genius or luck — it is relentless contact with reality and the willingness to persist through ambiguity.

## Key Frameworks & Models

### Customer Development (4-Step Process)
- **What it is**: The operating system for turning startup guesses into validated learning — everything on day one is a hypothesis.
- **When to use it**: From the moment you have a startup idea through to repeatable sales and company-building.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Customer Discovery** — Test whether the problem, customer, and value proposition are real by talking to potential buyers.
  2. **Customer Validation** — Prove there is a repeatable way to sell (not just one-off deals).
  3. **Customer Creation** — Create scalable demand once the sales model works.
  4. **Company Building** — Transition from a searching startup into an executing company.
  5. Apply the **72-hour rule**: aggregate customer feedback before reacting. One loud prospect is noise; repeated willingness to pay is signal.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is the problem real or am I projecting?
  - Can I sell this repeatably, or is every deal bespoke?
  - Am I hearing signal (multiple customers with checkbooks) or noise (one opinion)?
  - When should I pivot versus persevere?
- **Speaker source**: Steve Blank

### The Lean Methodology
- **What it is**: The combination of Customer Development + Agile Engineering + Business Model Canvas — the full operating framework Blank co-created.
- **When to use it**: When building anything venture-scale where assumptions vastly outnumber facts.
- **How it works**:
  1. Map all assumptions on a Business Model Canvas.
  2. Rank hypotheses by risk — test the riskiest first.
  3. Get out of the building — talk to 100+ customers (Blank's Stanford class talks to 100-125 in 10 weeks).
  4. Build MVPs iteratively, present what you learned each week.
  5. Pivot based on evidence, not ego.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What are my riskiest assumptions?
  - Which hypotheses, if wrong, kill the business?
  - Am I building what customers will pay for, or what I think is cool?
- **Speaker source**: Steve Blank

### Technology Adoption Lifecycle and Crossing the Chasm
- **What it is**: A go-to-market model explaining why many promising products stall between early enthusiasts and the mainstream market.
- **When to use it**: When you have early traction with visionaries but struggle to break into the mainstream.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Identify where you are**: Innovators (2.5%) -> Early Adopters (13.5%) -> [CHASM] -> Early Majority (34%) -> Late Majority (34%) -> Laggards (16%).
  2. **Find "pragmatists in pain"** — mainstream buyers with a business responsibility that is failing and getting worse. "This is not Disney, this is Dante. We're talking inferno."
  3. **Pick one use case in one industry** — commit for at least 3 quarters, go all in.
  4. **Deliver the whole product** — not just the core technology, but customer support, integrations, training, warranties, seamless end-to-end experience.
  5. **Use the Bowling Alley** — dominate one pin, then expand: either new use case in same industry OR same use case in different industry.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Are my current customers visionaries or pragmatists?
  - What is the specific broken process that pragmatists are suffering from?
  - Is my product a "whole product" or just a core technology?
  - Am I focused enough on one pin, or spraying across segments?
- **Speaker source**: Geoffrey Moore

### Trapped Value Analysis
- **What it is**: A diagnostic tool for finding where AI (or any technology) can create the most value inside an organization.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating business ideas, preparing for job interviews, or identifying where technology can unlock constrained performance.
- **How it works**:
  1. Look at any organization's operating model.
  2. Ask: "Why isn't it twice as effective as it is?"
  3. Identify the constraint — that is the **trapped value**.
  4. Determine whether your technology can release that trapped value.
  5. Build your pitch and product around freeing that value.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Where is the bottleneck in the customer's workflow?
  - What would 2x effectiveness look like?
  - Can I credibly release that trapped value?
- **Speaker source**: Geoffrey Moore

### Four Stages of Go-to-Market
- **What it is**: A maturity model for how companies evolve their revenue approach.
- **When to use it**: When deciding how to price, package, and deliver your product at each growth stage.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Projects** — Bespoke, one-off, visionary clients want exclusivity. Funds early learning.
  2. **Solutions** — Cross the chasm, focus on solving specific problems. $10M-$100M ARR.
  3. **Products** — Horizontal platform (like Windows, Oracle databases). Massive scale.
  4. **Services** — Consumption-based (tokens, not seats). The Salesforce model.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Am I at the right stage for my current pricing model?
  - Should I still be doing bespoke projects or have I earned the right to productize?
- **Speaker source**: Geoffrey Moore

### Beachhead Selection Criteria
- **What it is**: A three-part test for choosing your first target market segment.
- **When to use it**: When deciding which customer segment to attack first.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Big enough to matter** — A $10M business needs a $100M+ problem.
  2. **Small enough to lead** — Don't get lost in a huge segment where you're invisible.
  3. **Good fit with crown jewels** — Your technology must match the problem naturally.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this segment big enough to build a real business?
  - Can I realistically become the leader in this niche?
  - Does my technology naturally solve this problem, or am I forcing it?
- **Speaker source**: Geoffrey Moore ("Picking any pin is 80%. Anytime you focus on anything, you're gonna get more powerful.")

### Startup Foundations Hierarchy
- **What it is**: A sequencing model showing the order in which startups must build their layers — skip levels and the whole thing collapses.
- **When to use it**: When diagnosing why a startup is struggling or planning what to build next.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Founders** — Why are they doing this? How well do they work together?
  2. **Problem** — What customer problem are they solving? What is the value proposition?
  3. **Product** — The product that solves the problem.
  4. **Team** — The team you build on top.
  5. **Go-to-Market** — The machine to find customers reliably.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Are we trying to scale before the lower layers are stable?
  - Which layer is actually broken?
  - Have we earned the right to move to the next level?
- **Speaker source**: Michel Wolfe ("When startups fail because they scale prematurely, it's almost always because they've tried to race up this stack.")

### Founder-Market Fit Spectrum
- **What it is**: The degree to which a founder has legitimate domain expertise or earned insight into the problem they are solving.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating whether you (or a founder you are investing in) have the right to build this company.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Inherited fit**: "Growing up, my dad ran a factory and I saw how he couldn't fill orders..."
  2. **Career fit**: "I used to be an insurance broker. Now I'm building an AI-based insurance product."
  3. **Earned fit**: Like Stripe founders — "never worked in banking or payments, they were teenage coders" who went and opened a bank account and asked how payments work.
  4. **Fake fit**: You read a TechCrunch article and decided this market was hot. This does not survive diligence.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Why will this specific team win in this specific market?
  - Can this founder credibly speak to the customer's pain?
  - Has the founder earned the right through direct exposure or deep research?
- **Speaker source**: Michel Wolfe

### Founding Team Recipe
- **What it is**: The complementary skill set pattern that works best in founding teams.
- **When to use it**: When choosing a co-founder or evaluating a founding team.
- **How it works**:
  1. One person obsessed with customers (commercial).
  2. One person obsessed with product (technical).
  3. Similarity in values, but different skill sets.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Do we have both the customer-facing and product-building sides covered?
  - Are we too similar in skills?
  - Is the technical co-founder a true co-founder or a "distant #3"?
- **Speaker source**: Michel Wolfe (failure patterns: two technical founders with nobody talking to customers, or two business co-founders making the engineer a subordinate)

### Gas + Spark Fundraising Model
- **What it is**: A mental model for what creates fundraising momentum.
- **When to use it**: When preparing to raise a round and assessing your readiness.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Gas** = traction, metrics, progress, evidence of momentum.
  2. **Spark** = compelling story, vision, founder narrative.
  3. Both must be present for the fundraising "explosion." Gas without spark = boring metrics nobody cares about. Spark without gas = a nice story with no proof.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Do I have enough traction to back up my story?
  - Is my narrative compelling enough to ignite investor interest?
  - Am I missing one side of the equation?
- **Speaker source**: Danny King

### The Cockroach Survival Test
- **What it is**: The investor's test for founder resilience — will this person survive no matter what?
- **When to use it**: When evaluating your own commitment or when investors are testing your mettle.
- **How it works**:
  1. Can the founder survive with no salary? (Danny King took none for 6 years.)
  2. Can the founder endure years of rejection? (Danny King pitched 300 investors; 7 said yes.)
  3. Can the founder keep selling despite embarrassment? (Danny King accidentally emailed Google 14 times in one day — they became a customer.)
  4. Will the founder keep going when the business takes 4+ years to find product-market fit?
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this founder unkillable?
  - Can they survive the long plateau before product-market fit?
  - Do they have the irrational persistence needed?
- **Speaker source**: Danny King ("No salary for 6 years. Be a cockroach.")

### Seed VC Investment Filter
- **What it is**: The rapid-fire assessment seed VCs use to screen thousands of companies.
- **When to use it**: Before pitching a seed investor — run yourself through their filter first.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Team** — Need a strong CTO. Will pass without one.
  2. **Market** — Big enough for $500M-2B revenue potential.
  3. **Product** — Unique, differentiated, not just a wrapper around an LLM.
  4. **Commitment** — Founder must want to spend 10-15 years on this.
  5. **Preparation** — "An entrepreneur who doesn't have the deck ready is fucking lazy."
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Would a seed investor say no within 10 seconds?
  - Is the team technically strong enough?
  - Is the market big enough for venture returns?
- **Speaker source**: Jeff Clavier (10,000 referrals/year -> ~500 first meetings -> ~100 diligence -> 13 investments; rejection rate: 99.95%)

### Draper Associates Investment Criteria
- **What it is**: What a risk-tolerant seed VC with $600-700M AUM looks for.
- **When to use it**: When pitching to early-stage VCs or evaluating your own pitch readiness.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Founder Mission Alignment** — "If you're gonna build a unicorn, you gotta do it for 10 years." Opposite of "mercenary" founder chasing opportunity.
  2. **Market Size ($1-2B+)** — Bottom-up sizing, not ChatGPT numbers.
  3. **Clarity** — Should understand the business in 30 seconds. "I was shocked how many times I listened for 30 minutes and still don't know what they're doing."
  4. **Stage Fit** — Not too early (need hand-holding), not too late (already raised $20M).
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Can I explain my business in 30 seconds?
  - Is my founder story authentic (the "Batman origin story")?
  - Am I at the right stage for this investor?
- **Speaker source**: James Walker

### The Pitch Opening: Batman Origin Story
- **What it is**: A framework for the opening of a founder pitch that hooks investors emotionally.
- **When to use it**: In the first 60 seconds of any pitch to investors.
- **How it works**:
  1. Lead with your **Eureka moment** — the personal, often painful reason you care about this problem.
  2. Do NOT lead with credentials ("I did my PhD here").
  3. Make the investor feel why you are irrationally committed to solving this problem.
  4. Close the pitch with the **10-year vision** — when the current business works, what's the giant idea? Best version: this comes out naturally in Q&A, not forced into the deck.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Why does this founder care so deeply?
  - Is this commitment authentic or performed?
  - What is the massive endgame?
- **Speaker source**: James Walker ("I want the founder moment. Batman's parents getting murdered. Why do you care so much?")

### Bamboo Company Test
- **What it is**: A better north star than "unicorn" status — the metaphor VCs actually use internally.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating whether your company has the right growth profile for venture.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Grows fast** — fastest growing plant on the planet, grows an inch a day.
  2. **Needs little** — thrives without resources.
  3. **Is resilient** — incredibly hard to kill.
  4. **Lasts forever** — once established, cannot be eradicated.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is the company growing efficiently or burning capital to create growth theater?
  - Can it survive adverse conditions?
  - Does it have natural growth momentum or does every dollar of growth require a dollar of spend?
- **Speaker source**: Sean Foote ("Unicorns? Stupid, stupid idea. Bamboo companies." / "The highest possible growth with the least possible effort. That's what VCs are looking for.")

### VC Portfolio Math
- **What it is**: The brutal arithmetic that governs why VCs behave the way they do.
- **When to use it**: Before taking VC money — understand what their return model forces upon your company.
- **How it works**:
  1. Typical 10-company portfolio: 5 lose everything, 4 break even, 1 must return **46x in 5 years**.
  2. More than 50% of seed investments fail outright. (Jeff Clavier: 15 failures, 10 return some money, top 3-6 determine fund performance.)
  3. Most funds don't even return 1x. Many LPs signed up for 10 years; it's now year 15, still waiting. (James Walker)
  4. Each deal must be able to return at least half the fund. Steve Blank returned $1B each to two investors who put in $12M.
  5. To IPO today: need $500M revenue, 30% growth, profitable. Used to be $100M. (Jeff Clavier)
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Can my company plausibly return half the VC's fund?
  - Am I building at a scale that makes sense for venture capital?
  - Should I take VC money, or would alternative funding preserve more options?
- **Speaker source**: Sean Foote, Jeff Clavier, James Walker, Steve Blank ("If you take money from a VC, their business model becomes yours.")

### The Liquidity Crisis Framework
- **What it is**: Understanding why the exit environment matters as much as the fundraising environment.
- **When to use it**: When planning fundraising timing and exit strategy.
- **How it works**:
  1. "Investment - Liquidity Event = Charity" — No exit means the investment is worthless.
  2. 30,000 private companies need exits — the market is stuck. (Jeff Clavier)
  3. 2,000 unicorns on paper — many aren't unicorns anymore.
  4. Top quartile 2017 fund: only 14 cents on the dollar returned after 8 years. (Sean Foote)
  5. LPs are not deploying new capital because they haven't gotten returns from existing funds.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What is my realistic path to liquidity?
  - Is the exit market open for companies like mine?
  - Am I building toward an exit that buyers actually want?
- **Speaker source**: Sean Foote ("Investment minus liquidity event equals charity.")

### Inefficient Markets = Opportunity
- **What it is**: The investment thesis that venture returns come from information asymmetry, not efficient market participation.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating whether your company operates in a market with defensible information advantages.
- **How it works**:
  1. Efficient markets: everyone has the same information, returns follow a bell curve.
  2. VC is an inefficient market — not everyone sees every deal, creating long-tail returns.
  3. The startup's goal: find and exploit the inefficiency.
  4. Key test: **Do you have a data moat?** If yes, you're in an inefficient market.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What information advantage does my company have?
  - Can I build a defensible data position?
  - Am I competing in an efficient market where everyone has the same inputs?
- **Speaker source**: Sean Foote

### Distribution as Unfair Advantage
- **What it is**: The principle that product quality alone is insufficient — distribution is often the deciding edge between a niche tool and a category company.
- **When to use it**: When planning your go-to-market strategy or evaluating competitive positioning.
- **How it works**:
  1. Assess: is your product "cool" or is it a "massive company"? The difference is distribution.
  2. Build distribution into the product experience (e.g., GCAI founder runs Maven courses teaching GCs to use AI — the tool she teaches is her product, converts attendees to customers, positions her as expert, raised at $500M valuation).
  3. Test velocity constantly: "What's new?" is the killer question. If nothing's new, why not?
  4. Go mid-market first — avoid enterprise sales cycle death spiral early on.
  5. Never build custom features for a single enterprise design partner.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - How will we actually grow beyond our first 10 customers?
  - Do we have an unfair distribution channel?
  - Are we building for one design partner or for a market?
- **Speaker source**: Kathleen Estreich ("A cool product is a cool product. A cool product with strong distribution equals a massive company.")

### Board Composition and Investor Relationship Management
- **What it is**: Practical governance wisdom for how to build, run, and protect your board.
- **When to use it**: When taking on investors, structuring board seats, or running board meetings.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Ideal board size: 3-5 people** (plus CEO). When it grows too big, effectiveness drops.
  2. **Diversity of perspective**: have cheerleaders AND challengers. Both serve the company.
  3. **SV board culture**: "Our role is to help and support the entrepreneurs. We're not there for oversight." The one responsibility: if the CEO needs to be replaced, they will speak up. But that's last resort.
  4. **Due diligence on investors**: get references from people who have worked with them for an extended period. Have the VC partner over for dinner — "I just wanted to spend time with this person."
  5. **Run efficient meetings**: 5-10 minutes for updates, max. The rest should be "here are the possibilities, here's our analysis, the pros and cons." NOT: "We don't know what to do."
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is my board the right size and mix?
  - Am I presenting decisions to the board, or dumping problems on them?
  - Have I truly diligenced my investors as people?
- **Speaker source**: Ariel Poler ("Once you bring in a board member who represents an investor, there's no divorce. You're stuck.")

### Survival Path and Timing
- **What it is**: A way to think about timing as survivability, not prophecy.
- **When to use it**: When your idea is ahead of the market, or when evaluating whether to enter an emerging space.
- **How it works**:
  1. Find a path that keeps you alive until the market catches up. (Ariel Poler's Limitless: started with Zoom transcription, built incrementally toward the full vision.)
  2. Focus on the specific painful process where customers need relief NOW. (Geoffrey Moore)
  3. Use iterative validation rather than grand predictions. (Steve Blank)
  4. If the mission is right but the model is wrong, pivot the model, not the mission. (Mike Kuczynski: clothing failed, carbon credits succeeded — same conservation mission.)
  5. Two of the smartest VCs (Doerr, McNamee) missed the web in 1993. Within six months they changed their minds. Timing uncertainty is universal.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Can I survive until the market is ready?
  - What is my intermediate product that generates revenue while I wait?
  - Is the mission right even if the current model is wrong?
- **Speaker source**: Ariel Poler, Geoffrey Moore, Steve Blank, Mike Kuczynski

### Segmentation and Native Value
- **What it is**: A growth and product lens built around user cohorts and native platform behavior.
- **When to use it**: When building product features, ad products, or growth strategies for consumer or platform businesses.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Segment users into cohorts** — L1 of 7 (comes once a week) vs L5 of 7 (highly engaged) need completely different strategies.
  2. **Build on native value** — Facebook = discovery/prospecting; Google = search/intent. "If we tried to do the same kind of ads that work on Google on Facebook, it would not work."
  3. **Start simple** — Facebook's early algorithm was a simple weighted sum (Comments = 100x likes, Shares = 10x likes). "Because the network was sparse, it is so easy for something simple to work really well."
  4. **Aggregate thinking hides truth** — "Segmentation and understanding what each of the cohorts need is very important because trying to think about it in an aggregate almost never works."
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Which user cohort matters most right now?
  - Does my monetization strategy match my product's native value?
  - Am I using one approach for all users when I should be segmenting?
- **Speaker source**: Asad Awan

### AI Market Map (2025)
- **What it is**: A practitioner view of where value is actually forming in AI right now.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating AI startup ideas or investment opportunities.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Three types of AI**: Predictive (mature, 30-40 years old, embedded everywhere), Generative (in the "tornado" — everyone buying at once), Agentic (at the chasm — human removal is the key difference). (Geoffrey Moore)
  2. **Platform not settled**: We're in browser wars phase — no winner yet. The operating layer will be agents on top of language models. (Steve Blank)
  3. **Most AI startups are just pilots**: "I've talked to CIOs who say this year: do whatever you want, try five AI pilots. Next year we're gonna cancel four of them." (Michel Wolfe)
  4. **Two worlds**: AI companies raising easily at high valuations vs non-AI companies struggling. "If somebody tells you it's a bubble, they're probably on the non-AI side." (Michel Wolfe)
  5. **For first time ever, AI was more than half of all venture investing.** 70-80 billion raised by just 5 companies. "It's not actually venture capital anymore. It's project finance." (Sean Foote)
  6. **Harvey (AI for law) already suffering** as LLMs improve — commodification risk is real. (Michel Wolfe)
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Which type of AI am I building (predictive, generative, agentic)?
  - Am I building a pilot or a product?
  - Where is my structural moat if the model layer commoditizes?
- **Speaker source**: Geoffrey Moore, Steve Blank, Michel Wolfe, Sean Foote

### Technical vs Adaptive Challenges
- **What it is**: A distinction between problems with known solutions and problems requiring leadership transformation.
- **When to use it**: When diagnosing what kind of help a business actually needs.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Technical challenges**: Known solution exists, need right expertise/software. "I need a new CRM."
  2. **Adaptive challenges**: No obvious answer, can't just look it up. "I had 80% market share, now 3 new competitors, what do I do?"
  3. You can hire McKinsey for technical challenges (but pay 20x more).
  4. The biggest adaptive "aha moment": "I realize now that the biggest problem for my business is me."
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is my problem technical (solvable with expertise) or adaptive (requires me to change)?
  - Am I hiring consultants for problems that require self-transformation?
- **Speaker source**: Darius Teter (Stanford Seed)

### SME Growth in Emerging Markets
- **What it is**: A framework for understanding entrepreneurship where venture capital doesn't exist and growth must be self-financed.
- **When to use it**: When building or advising businesses in developing economies, or when comparing SV assumptions to global reality.
- **How it works**:
  1. **98% of all businesses** in most countries are SMEs (1-249 employees). For every 1 job created in large companies, 1.6-2 jobs created in small enterprises.
  2. In emerging markets, there is no "unlimited capital for great ideas" — you must finance through own revenues, customer by customer, dollar by dollar.
  3. **Capital is no longer the issue in Africa — it's pipeline** and corruption. Can you enforce a contract? Can you get resolution in courts?
  4. **Human Capital Strategy** is the biggest constraint: finding quality 2nd-level managers. Everyone tries to steal your good people.
  5. **Alumni networks are the real value**: 42% of Stanford Seed alumni do business with each other. WhatsApp "Needs and Leads" channels produce answers in an hour.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is my growth strategy realistic without venture capital?
  - What is the actual bottleneck: capital, talent, or institutional trust?
  - Can I build a network that creates compounding value?
- **Speaker source**: Darius Teter (Stanford Seed — one Indian company went from $8-9M to $400M annual turnover through the program)

### Platform Risk Assessment
- **What it is**: The structural risk of building on someone else's platform or inside someone else's rules.
- **When to use it**: Whenever your business depends on a platform owner (Apple, Google, OpenAI, regulators).
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify every platform dependency. Ask: which external platform, regulator, or channel partner can damage the business?
  2. Apple can wipe out an entire business model overnight — Tapjoy had 100% of revenue eliminated when Apple banned their ad unit. (Paul Longhenry)
  3. GDPR functions as a moat for incumbents by raising experimentation costs for startups. "Unless you were as well funded as TikTok, literally nothing else can grow in Europe that easily." (Asad Awan)
  4. Big tech moves slowly but owns distribution, data infrastructure, and embedded positions. Internal tools at Google/YouTube become startup ideas for ex-employees. (Jordan Gomes)
  5. Assess your hedge: what happens if the platform changes the rules?
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Can my business survive a platform policy change?
  - Am I building on a platform that benefits from keeping me alive, or one that might compete with me?
  - What is my hedge?
- **Speaker source**: Paul Longhenry, Asad Awan, Jordan Gomes

### Earnings Arbitrage and Cross-Border Exit Strategy
- **What it is**: The insight that different capital markets value the same business at wildly different multiples.
- **When to use it**: When planning exits, choosing acquirers, or evaluating international M&A opportunities.
- **How it works**:
  1. Chinese capital markets historically paid 15x earnings vs US buyers who wouldn't. (Michael Levit sold browser business for $252M using this arbitrage.)
  2. European markets are "too rational" — exits are harder because acquirers are disciplined. (Sean Foote: "My entire career depends on irrational acquirers.")
  3. Hotmail was acquired for $400M with no revenue — possible only with irrational buyer dynamics. (Sean Foote)
  4. If you want to raise money from the US, you probably need US revenues. (Sean Foote)
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Who are the potential acquirers and what multiples do they pay?
  - Am I building in a market where irrational acquirers exist?
  - Should I target a different geography for my exit?
- **Speaker source**: Michael Levit, Sean Foote

### For-Profit Impact Model
- **What it is**: The case that for-profit structures can outperform nonprofits for social impact when market incentives align.
- **When to use it**: When building a mission-driven business and choosing between nonprofit and for-profit structure.
- **How it works**:
  1. Nonprofits can't always innovate because their donors give money for free — why would they generate revenue? (Mike Kuczynski)
  2. Communities don't want charity — they want to earn money by providing value. (Mike Kuczynski)
  3. Pivot the model when the mission is right but the mechanism is wrong. Wildlife Works: clothing failed (retail couldn't tell the story), carbon credits succeeded ($200M peak revenue).
  4. Build 30-40 year contracts with communities for stability.
  5. Critics say "putting a price tag on nature" — but nature already has a price tag from deforestation. The question is: will the price tag for keeping it up beat the price tag for cutting it down?
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Would my impact be greater with a for-profit or nonprofit structure?
  - Does the market mechanism I'm choosing align with how my beneficiaries want to participate?
  - Can I create a durable business model, not just a fundraising model?
- **Speaker source**: Mike Kuczynski (Wildlife Works — 2,500 employees, 200,000 hectares protected, 120,000 community members)

### The "Enough Is Enough" Anti-Growth Model
- **What it is**: The philosophy that not every business should maximize growth, and that restraint can create more value than expansion.
- **When to use it**: When deciding whether to take VC money, sell to a platform, or "enshittify" a working product.
- **How it works**:
  1. Craigslist: $700M revenue with ~50 people. Hasn't changed in 25+ years.
  2. Craig Newmark declined billions from VCs.
  3. Chose not to "enshittify" the site.
  4. Simplicity wins — if the product works for the community, don't break it for growth.
  5. "Know when enough is enough" — Sunday school values, not MBA optimization.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Am I growing because growth creates value, or because I'm supposed to?
  - What would happen if I chose to stay small and profitable?
  - Is VC money the right path, or would it force me to destroy what works?
- **Speaker source**: Craig Newmark ("I declined billions. Didn't want to enshittify the site.")

### McKinsey's Three Horizons (Applied to VC Fundability)
- **What it is**: A strategic framework reinterpreted through VC appetite.
- **When to use it**: When deciding whether your idea is fundable by Silicon Valley VCs.
- **How it works**:
  1. **H1**: Make existing thing better — Harder to fund with VC.
  2. **H2**: Low cost/niche of existing market — Harder to fund with VC.
  3. **H3**: Something that never existed before (sounds insane) — This is what VCs want.
  4. "Right now most Silicon Valley VCs aren't funding Horizon 1 & 2. There's a line at their door of big things."
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is my idea H1, H2, or H3?
  - Am I pitching incremental improvement to VCs who want paradigm shifts?
  - Should I seek different funding for an H1/H2 business?
- **Speaker source**: Steve Blank

### Taxonomy of Entrepreneurs
- **What it is**: Not all entrepreneurs are the same — the type determines the right funding, strategy, and expectations.
- **When to use it**: When deciding what kind of entrepreneur you are and what funding model fits.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Lifestyle** — Work to support a passion (surfing, travel). Not VC-compatible.
  2. **Small Business** — 99.5% of US businesses, can make millions. Not VC-compatible.
  3. **Startup** — Early stage, still small. Maybe VC-compatible.
  4. **Scalable Startup** — Silicon Valley-style, aims for massive scale. VC-compatible.
  5. **Corporate/Government** — Different game entirely.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What type of entrepreneur am I?
  - Is VC the right funding for my type?
  - Am I building a lifestyle business while pitching like it's a scalable startup?
- **Speaker source**: Steve Blank

### Moonshot Project Evaluation
- **What it is**: How Google X evaluates radical innovation projects — relevant for anyone building ambitious technology.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating whether to pursue a breakthrough technology project or when building an innovation portfolio.
- **How it works**:
  1. Start with massive problems that affect millions.
  2. Require a breakthrough technology approach (not incremental improvement).
  3. Build small teams with non-engineering talent alongside engineers (Laura Thomson was early non-engineering hire at Google X).
  4. Projects that spun out: Waymo (self-driving), Wing (delivery drones — now multi-billion dollar), smart contact lens (licensed to Novartis).
  5. Not every moonshot works — but the ones that do create entirely new industries.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this problem big enough for a moonshot approach?
  - Do I need a breakthrough, or would incremental improvement work?
  - What expertise beyond engineering does this project need?
- **Speaker source**: Laura Thomson (Google X / Meta Reality Labs)

### Getting into Big Tech (Career Framework)
- **What it is**: Practical tactics for breaking into large technology companies, especially from non-traditional backgrounds.
- **When to use it**: When targeting a career at a major tech company.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Be intentional** — "Make a decision and be serious about it. People can feel if your heart isn't into it."
  2. **Contact decision makers directly** — "HR is a service provider to a decision maker." More than half of interesting jobs are never posted publicly.
  3. **Follow up relentlessly** — "People have inbox overflow. You need to be unstoppable."
  4. **Build network as investment** — Message people before you need a job. "Can we chat for 20 minutes about what you're doing?"
  5. **50% of candidates do zero preparation** — Minimal effort puts you ahead of half the field.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Am I approaching the decision maker or just HR?
  - Have I built relationships before I need them?
  - Am I treating the job search as a sales process?
- **Speaker source**: Jordan Gomes ("50% of interviewees have no idea why they're in the interview. If you do the minimum to show interest, you're way ahead.")

### The Six-Element Pitch Framework
- **What it is**: Every startup pitch contains the same six elements — and they map to a relationship pitch.
- **When to use it**: When structuring any pitch to investors.
- **How it works**:
  1. **Giant customer pain** — "You're unhappy and lonely."
  2. **Our solution** — "We should be together."
  3. **Better than competitors** — "I'm better than your ex."
  4. **Right team** — "I'm a good person, funny."
  5. **Great economics** — "The future is great."
  6. **Progress** — "Look how far we've come."
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Am I hitting all six elements?
  - Which element is weakest in my pitch?
  - Am I leading with pain (element 1) or burying it?
- **Speaker source**: Sean Foote

## Mental Models & Principles

### On Founding and Commitment
- **Everything starts as a guess**. Replace opinions with repeated customer evidence. (Steve Blank)
- **Founding is a calling, not a job.** Closest job to a founder is artist — sees things others don't, creates against all odds. "Normal people get jobs. Crazy people start companies." (Steve Blank)
- **Anger can be better fuel than excitement.** Two types of entrepreneurs: those excited about something, and those pissed off about something. Danny King's anger about broken credentialing fueled 13 years of building. (Danny King)
- **Your first company doesn't matter.** "You're gonna do five or ten more companies in your career. You get multiple shots at goal." (Steve Blank)
- **In Silicon Valley, the word for failed entrepreneur is "Experienced."** Failure here gets you more shots at goal. (Steve Blank)
- **Dysfunctional family backgrounds** can be an advantage — comfort with chaos and uncertainty. Not a requirement, but a pattern. (Steve Blank)
- **The company is your resume.** "I don't care about your resume. I care about what you've accomplished." Until you start, you're not an entrepreneur. (Sean Foote)

### On Markets and Timing
- **Pain sells faster than possibility.** Focus on pragmatists in pain, not broad abstract demand. (Geoffrey Moore)
- **The first market must be narrow enough to win.** Beachheads beat broad launches. "Picking any pin is 80%." (Geoffrey Moore)
- **The company must survive long enough for timing to work.** "What I look for is less about timing it right but having a path that lets me survive until the market is ready." (Ariel Poler)
- **Two of the smartest VCs missed the web.** Doerr said "interactive television." McNamee said "AOL." Within six months they changed their minds. Smart people are wrong about timing constantly. (Ariel Poler)
- **Geography determines your happiness.** "Your company is gonna take five to twelve years. Think long term." (Michel Wolfe)
- **Be where capital, talent, AND customers are.** Founders need to be on the ground. "Who are the best salespeople? Founders. Who are the first salespeople? Founders." (Steve Blank)

### On Product and Distribution
- **Distribution is part of the product.** Product strength without GTM strength is not enough. (Kathleen Estreich)
- **If the founder cannot explain it clearly, the company probably cannot sell it clearly.** Should understand business in 30 seconds. (James Walker)
- **Build on native value.** Products and ads must match the native experience of the platform. (Asad Awan)
- **Simple systems can outperform complex ones** when they match user behavior and product stage. Facebook's weighted sum worked because the network was sparse. (Asad Awan)
- **Innovation dies in committee.** Structured organizations produce consistent decisions but kill speed. (Jordan Gomes on YouTube)
- **Internal tools = startup opportunities.** If a big tech company builds an internal tool, mid-market companies need it too. (Jordan Gomes)
- **Entrepreneurial skills transfer well to big tech.** Systems thinking is rare and valued — entrepreneurial background leads to faster promotion. (Jordan Gomes)

### On Capital and Investors
- **VCs are not giving you money to be nice; they need outsized returns.** Their business model becomes yours. (Steve Blank, Jeff Clavier)
- **Bamboo beats unicorn theater.** Growth quality and resilience matter more than paper valuation. (Sean Foote)
- **Strong founder-market fit can be inherited or earned, but it cannot be faked for long.** (Michel Wolfe, James Walker)
- **No investor divorce.** Once investors have board influence, reversing that decision is near-impossible. (Ariel Poler)
- **People working for equity = strong signal to investors.** "Looking at all these smart people willing to work for equity for you, I figured you might be onto something." (Ariel Poler, quoting Tench Coxe of Sutter Hill)
- **Stock-only deals are dangerous.** Always get some cash. Michael Levit took all stock from Kmart ($31M deal), Kmart went bankrupt, net worth zero at 32. (Michael Levit)
- **Know when enough is enough.** Not every business should maximize growth at all costs. (Craig Newmark)
- **Counter-cyclical moves can be the best moves.** Jeff Clavier became a VC in 2002 when everyone was leaving after the dot-com crash. (Jeff Clavier)
- **VCs are bad at succession planning** — they don't want to let go of economics. (Jeff Clavier)
- **Talk to 10 VCs who know your market, not 300 random ones.** Quality over quantity in fundraising. (Paul Longhenry)
- **Red flag: "Nothing keeps me up at night."** VCs want founders who share problems so VCs can help. (Paul Longhenry)

### On AI Specifically
- **In AI, real advantage sits in workflow ownership, native value, and data loops.** (Asad Awan, Paul Longhenry, Michel Wolfe)
- **"When you stop saying 'amazing' about AI, that's when we start having the real conversation."** (Steve Blank)
- **"Everything about enterprise software is wrong"** — opportunity everywhere. (Steve Blank)
- **A lot of AI companies are really just pilots.** CIOs will try five AI pilots this year and cancel four next year. (Michel Wolfe)
- **Zero to $100M startups can now do it with 15 employees. Old days that was 200.** (Michel Wolfe)
- **AI coding tools make velocity table stakes.** If you're not using the latest tools, you'll be left behind. (Kathleen Estreich)
- **AI is productivity.** US productivity growth was 2.2%/year long-term; 2024 was 5% because of AI. (Sean Foote)
- **"Every single person in your company has to use this all day."** (Michel Wolfe)
- **OpenAI as a product is fundamentally different** — "much more trust centric" with "way more direct connection with the user" than social media platforms. (Asad Awan)
- **Choice and consent** should be core principles for AI product development. "Value is genuine value." (Asad Awan)

### On Mission and Values
- **Mission matters, but the business model still has to work.** (Mike Kuczynski — 25 years iterating on conservation business models)
- **For-profit can outperform nonprofit for impact** when market incentives align. Communities don't want charity; they want to earn. (Mike Kuczynski)
- **Get out of the way** when you have good people. Craig Newmark became "customer service representative, not a leader." (Craig Newmark)
- **People-focused leadership** is a philosophy, not a platitude. Michael Levit went from net worth zero at 32 to a $252M exit by building around people. (Michael Levit)
- **Corruption is the underlying problem in many markets.** Can you enforce a contract? Can you get resolution in courts? Infrastructure and institutions matter. (Darius Teter)

### On Career Strategy
- **Sales or Customer Success are the best entry points.** "Both put you in front of a customer who is skeptical, sometimes hostile. That's where you separate the story from reality." (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Stay fresh by stapling yourself to problems you don't know the answer to.** "And my customer cares about." (Geoffrey Moore)
- **"It's not about job titles you've had, but things you've done."** (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Join a startup before starting one.** "Unbelievable learning, build your network, build your knowledge." (Michel Wolfe)
- **Don't start with finance or strategy.** "It all starts with getting good at something — sales, marketing, product, engineering." (Michel Wolfe)
- **Chief of staff can be good** — "shadow a great CEO for a couple years." (Michel Wolfe)
- **"Go work at great companies where people are really smart and you'll be in rooms you have no business being in."** (Kathleen Estreich)
- **Time > Capital.** Where you spend time matters more than money. Evaluate companies like an investor evaluates your time. (Kathleen Estreich)
- **The Coffee Test:** Get the offer, then ask your new boss out for coffee. If they won't take the time, they won't give you the time once you work there. (Kathleen Estreich)
- **The interview process is usually indicative of how they're gonna treat you once you join.** (Kathleen Estreich)
- **Golden handcuffs are real.** "Free food, good salary, no weekends... Life is so good, why leave?" (Jordan Gomes)

## Common Mistakes & Anti-Patterns

### Product and Market Mistakes
- **Building for a customer who is interested but not ready to change or pay.** Opinions are not demand. "If you would do X" isn't valuable. Need: "I'll write you a check right now." (Steve Blank, Geoffrey Moore)
- **Targeting too many segments at once** and never establishing a beachhead. Most startups die because they try to serve too many segments before they have a credible one. (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Pitching "AI for X" without a structural edge**, proprietary data, or workflow ownership. Every market is now "AI for X" with twenty companies doing each. (Jeff Clavier, Paul Longhenry, Michel Wolfe)
- **Building a wrapper around an LLM** and calling it a company. No deck = lazy, but a thin wrapper = worse. (Jeff Clavier)
- **Treating design partners like proof of a business** when they are really just expensive custom projects. "If you have one design partner telling you to build custom stuff, you're basically an extension of GE, not building a company." (Kathleen Estreich)
- **Feature-focused marketing instead of problem-focused marketing.** Engineering founders say "Our thing does X, Y, Z" when they should say "Our thing will relieve your stress." (Darius Teter)
- **Assuming pilots equal adoption.** "A lot of AI companies are really just pilots." CIOs will cancel most of them. (Michel Wolfe)
- **Equating interest, LOIs, demos, or curiosity with durable adoption.** (Michel Wolfe, Steve Blank)

### Fundraising and Financial Mistakes
- **Optimizing for vanity valuation** rather than investor quality and board chemistry. Ariel Poler took lower valuation from Sequoia because the partner value was better. (Ariel Poler, Sean Foote)
- **Ignoring dilution, control rights, and the long-term cost of the wrong term sheet.** 51% common ownership does not guarantee practical control; acquirers want 99%, minority holders can block. (Ariel Poler, Steve Blank)
- **Taking all stock, no cash.** Michael Levit: $31M deal, all stock, Kmart goes bankrupt, net worth zero at 32. (Michael Levit)
- **The opposite mistake: taking all cash when you should take stock.** Michael Levit sold company to Alibaba (150 people at the time), took all cash, refused stock. Missed out on billions in Alibaba growth. (Michael Levit)
- **Not understanding VC math before taking VC money.** "If you can't draw a VC's financial model on a whiteboard, you'll be the ex-CEO." (Steve Blank)
- **Hiding problems from investors.** VCs want founders who share problems so they can help. "Nothing keeps me up at night" is a red flag. (Paul Longhenry)
- **The Liar Test.** Founder said "we say we have 3 million users." Pushed: actual number was 800K. "He couldn't turn off the sales gene. We're already investors!" Result: didn't invest more. (Sean Foote)
- **Believing the deck matters more than preparation.** Investors see thousands of companies and filter in seconds. "An entrepreneur who doesn't have the deck ready is fucking lazy." (Jeff Clavier)
- **Pitching 300 random VCs** instead of 10 who know your market. (Paul Longhenry)

### Scaling and Operations Mistakes
- **Premature scaling** — hiring sales, scaling GTM, or adding management layers before founder, problem, and product layers are solid. "Almost always because they've tried to race up this stack." (Michel Wolfe)
- **Running enterprise sales before having a sharp wedge or enough speed to survive the cycle.** Enterprise sales takes years; one person can derail an entire deal. Go mid-market first. (Kathleen Estreich)
- **Thrashing engineering with every customer request.** Apply the 72-hour rule — aggregate feedback, look for patterns. (Steve Blank)
- **Not having a CTO.** Jeff Clavier will pass on CEO + product person + biz person with no technical co-founder. Two business co-founders making the engineer a "distant #3" is a failure pattern. (Jeff Clavier, Michel Wolfe)
- **Failing late.** "If you fail early it's fine. If you fail late, that really hurts." Late failures consume more capital and more of a VC fund's reserves. (Jeff Clavier)

### Mindset Mistakes
- **Founders give up before they run out of money.** Grit determines survival more than funding. (Kathleen Estreich)
- **Chasing growth for growth's sake** instead of deciding what "enough" means. (Craig Newmark)
- **Assuming platform dependence is safe** because the relationship looks stable today. Apple killed 100% of Tapjoy's business overnight. (Paul Longhenry)
- **Leading pitches with credentials** ("I did my PhD here") instead of the founder moment. (James Walker)
- **Over-planning instead of jumping.** "Don't plan, don't over plan. Don't think you need to know what's around the next corner." (Jeff Clavier)
- **European risk aversion.** "French will do anything to make a lot of money. They will do everything to avoid losing money. There's a big difference in how you approach risk." (Jeff Clavier)
- **Not being rigid enough about focus, not flexible enough about approach.** Sean Foote tests this by interrupting rudely during pitches to see how founders react. Rigid = bad. Flexible = good. (Sean Foote)
- **Avoiding sectors because they're "unsexy."** Best founder stories are often boring domains: "I worked in insurance, we weren't doing it right, so I decided to do it myself." (James Walker)

## How This Discipline Challenges Others

Silicon Valley practitioner thinking challenges other disciplines by forcing them into contact with market reality:

- **Challenges academic strategy** by asking whether the plan survives first contact with customers. "No business plan survives first contact with customers." (Steve Blank)
- **Challenges product thinking** by asking whether anyone will actually pay, renew, and refer — not just use, like, or express interest.
- **Challenges finance** by reminding that the wrong capital can destroy an otherwise good company. Venture money is not neutral — it changes growth expectations, time horizons, risk profiles, and the CEO's room to maneuver.
- **Challenges innovation theater** by demanding a narrow wedge, not abstract novelty. "This is not Disney, this is Dante." (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Challenges operations** by insisting that sequencing matters: sell, learn, and validate before scaling. Don't race up the startup stack. (Michel Wolfe)
- **Challenges ethics-light growth thinking** by reminding that control, board dynamics, platform dependence, and user trust all have real consequences.
- **Challenges MBA frameworks** by valuing earned insight over theoretical analysis. "It's not about job titles you've had, but things you've done." (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Challenges European caution** by pointing out that risk avoidance is itself a risk. Counter-cyclical moves (Jeff Clavier becoming a VC during dot-com crash) and irrational commitment (Danny King's 6 years without salary) are features, not bugs.
- **Challenges nonprofit impact models** by showing that for-profit structures can deliver more durable impact when incentives align with beneficiary desires. Communities want to earn, not receive charity. (Mike Kuczynski)
- **Challenges big-company thinking** by showing that "innovation dies in committee" (Jordan Gomes) and simplicity can run $700M in revenue with 50 people (Craig Newmark).
- **Challenges development economics** by arguing the bottleneck in emerging markets is not capital but pipeline, talent, institutions, and the founder themselves. "I realize now that the biggest problem for my business is me." (Darius Teter)

## Key Vocabulary & Concepts

- **Customer Discovery**: Testing whether the problem and buyer are real before scaling. (Steve Blank)
- **Customer Validation**: Proving there is a repeatable sales motion. (Steve Blank)
- **Whole Product**: The complete solution a pragmatist needs — not just core technology but support, integrations, training, warranties. (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Pragmatists in Pain**: Mainstream buyers with a failing process and budget urgency. "Their back is against the wall." (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Beachhead Market**: The first narrow segment you can realistically dominate. (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Bowling Alley**: Sequential expansion from one winning segment into adjacent ones. (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Trapped Value**: The constraint preventing an organization from being twice as effective. (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Forward Deployed Engineer**: Engineers in customer environment who can write code directly into product. Salesforce model. (Geoffrey Moore)
- **Founder-Market Fit**: Why this founder has earned the right to solve this problem — inherited, career-based, or earned through research. (Michel Wolfe, James Walker)
- **Startup Foundations Stack**: Founders -> Problem -> Product -> Team -> GTM -> Company Operations. Skip levels at your peril. (Michel Wolfe)
- **Gas + Spark**: Traction plus compelling story; both required for fundraising ignition. (Danny King)
- **Cockroach Mentality**: The founder's willingness to survive at all costs — no salary, endless rejection, irrational persistence. (Danny King)
- **Bamboo Company**: Fast-growing, resilient, capital-efficient business. Better north star than unicorn. (Sean Foote)
- **Data Moat**: Structural advantage created by proprietary data or better feedback loops. (Paul Longhenry, Sean Foote)
- **Platform Risk**: Dependence on Apple, Google, OpenAI, regulators, or other gatekeepers who can change the rules. (Paul Longhenry)
- **Pilot Purgatory**: Apparent demand that never turns into durable budget or rollout. Most AI pilots will be cancelled. (Michel Wolfe)
- **No Investor Divorce**: Once investors have board influence, reversing that decision is near-impossible. (Ariel Poler)
- **Survival Path**: The concrete route that lets a startup stay alive until timing improves. (Ariel Poler)
- **Native Value**: The inherent user job or behavior the product naturally supports. Ads must match. (Asad Awan)
- **L3/L5/L7 of 7**: User engagement cohorts measuring days active per week. Different cohorts need different strategies. (Asad Awan)
- **Technical vs Adaptive Challenges**: Known-solution problems vs. problems requiring leadership transformation. (Darius Teter)
- **Human Capital Strategy**: Finding and retaining quality 2nd-level managers — the biggest SME constraint. (Darius Teter)
- **REDD+**: Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation — the carbon credit framework for protecting standing forests. (Mike Kuczynski)
- **Enshittification**: Degrading a product to extract more value from users. Craig Newmark's explicit reason for declining VC money. (Craig Newmark)
- **Three Horizons (VC lens)**: H1 (improve existing) and H2 (niche of existing) are harder to fund; H3 (never existed, sounds insane) is what VCs want. (Steve Blank)
- **The Batman Origin Story**: The pitch opening that hooks investors — why do you care so deeply about this problem? (James Walker)
- **The Liar Test**: Investors catch founders hedging on metrics. "We say we have 3 million users" vs actual 800K. (Sean Foote)
- **The Coffee Test**: After getting a job offer, ask your new boss for coffee. If they won't take the time, they won't give you the time once you work there. (Kathleen Estreich)
- **Earnings Arbitrage**: Different capital markets value the same business at different multiples. Can create exit opportunities. (Michael Levit)
- **Counter-cyclical entry**: Making career or investment moves when everyone else is leaving. Jeff Clavier became a VC during the dot-com crash. (Jeff Clavier)

## Decision Criteria for Business Ideas

### Pursue if:
- There is a clear painful wedge for a specific customer segment, not just generic interest. Find pragmatists in pain. (Geoffrey Moore)
- The founding team has real founder-market fit — inherited, career-based, or earned through deep research. (Michel Wolfe)
- The company can explain the product, buyer, and GTM in plain language within 30 seconds. (James Walker)
- Early customer evidence shows repeated willingness to pay, not just curiosity. "I'll write you a check right now" beats "If you would do X." (Steve Blank)
- The first market is narrow enough to win and large enough to matter. $10M business needs $100M+ problem. (Geoffrey Moore)
- The product can become a whole solution for a beachhead segment — not just core technology. (Geoffrey Moore)
- Distribution is credible and does not rely on magical virality. The founder has an unfair distribution channel. (Kathleen Estreich)
- The business can survive long enough for the market to mature. There is a survival path with intermediate revenue. (Ariel Poler)
- In AI, the company owns workflow, data, distribution, or implementation leverage — not only model access. (Paul Longhenry, Asad Awan, Michel Wolfe)
- The fundraising plan fits the company type, and the founder understands VC math and its consequences. (Steve Blank, Sean Foote)
- The founding team has one person obsessed with customers and one obsessed with product. (Michel Wolfe)
- The company has "bamboo qualities" — can grow fast with minimal resources and is hard to kill. (Sean Foote)
- The founder has the "cockroach" mentality — will survive at all costs through years of ambiguity. (Danny King)
- There is a structural data advantage or information asymmetry the company can exploit. (Sean Foote, Paul Longhenry)

### Pass or redesign if:
- The company is trying to sell to everyone at once with no beachhead. (Geoffrey Moore)
- The wedge depends on customers changing behavior without pain or urgency. (Steve Blank, Geoffrey Moore)
- The story is really "we are an AI wrapper" with no defensible asset. Twenty companies are doing each "AI for X." (Jeff Clavier, Michel Wolfe)
- The company needs enterprise sales discipline before it has product clarity or GTM speed. (Kathleen Estreich)
- The economics depend on investors staying excited rather than customers staying committed. (Michel Wolfe — "a lot of AI companies are really just pilots")
- The founder is optimizing for valuation while ignoring dilution, board dynamics, or partner quality. (Ariel Poler, Sean Foote)
- The business can be killed by one platform policy change with no hedge. Apple killed 100% of Tapjoy overnight. (Paul Longhenry)
- The team is scaling functions that sit above unresolved founder, problem, or product issues. Racing up the stack before foundations are solid. (Michel Wolfe)
- The company has no credible path to survive until market timing works. (Ariel Poler)
- The founder sounds impressive but cannot answer the operator's question: what actually happens when you try to sell it, raise for it, and scale it?
- The founding team is two business people with no technical co-founder. (Jeff Clavier, Michel Wolfe)
- The founder leads with credentials instead of conviction. (James Walker)
- The founder hides problems from investors instead of sharing them. (Paul Longhenry, Sean Foote)
- The company equates being in the AI space with having a business. "Sixteen out of sixteen AI — that's why they raised so quickly" — but raising is not winning. (Michel Wolfe)
- There is no path to liquidity. "Investment minus liquidity event equals charity." (Sean Foote)
- The company exists in a market with only rational acquirers and no irrational buyers. (Sean Foote)
- The founder has not studied pitch decks, watched demo days, or immersed themselves in the startup ecosystem. "Very few people actually do it." (Michel Wolfe)
