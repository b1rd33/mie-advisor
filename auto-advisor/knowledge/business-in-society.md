# Business in Society - Distilled Knowledge

## Core Philosophy
Business in Society treats the firm as a social and ecological actor, not just a profit machine. Its core claim is that businesses create value inside broader systems made of people, institutions, ecosystems, and shared resources. That means a business model is never judged only by revenue growth or margins, but by who benefits, who pays, which externalities are created, which stakeholders are harmed or helped, and whether the model remains viable once full social and environmental costs are considered. The discipline pushes managers from "less bad" thinking toward system-aware, stakeholder-centered, and eventually regenerative forms of value creation.

## Key Frameworks & Models

### Purpose of the Firm Debate
- **What it is**: The foundational tension between shareholder value maximization and stakeholder value creation.
- **When to use it**: When defining company purpose, governance priorities, or what "success" means.
- **How it works**:
  1. Compare the shareholder lens: profit for owners is the objective.
  2. Compare the stakeholder lens: create long-term value for all affected parties.
  3. Test which lens is driving strategic choices, trade-offs, and metrics.
  4. Check whether social or environmental issues are treated as core value questions or as side constraints.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What is this business fundamentally for?
  - Who counts in decision-making?
  - Are we optimizing profit alone or durable stakeholder value?

### Stakeholder Theory
- **What it is**: A framework for identifying everyone who affects or is affected by the organization.
- **When to use it**: In strategy, risk assessment, sustainability design, and partnership mapping.
- **How it works**:
  1. List primary stakeholders: employees, customers, suppliers, investors, communities.
  2. List secondary stakeholders: NGOs, associations, regulators, competitors, media.
  3. Map what each stakeholder gives, receives, risks, and bears.
  4. Evaluate where interests align, conflict, or are ignored.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Who has a legitimate claim on this business?
  - Who captures value and who absorbs cost?
  - Which neglected stakeholders create future risk?

### Triple Bottom Line
- **What it is**: A performance lens that evaluates People, Planet, and Profit together.
- **When to use it**: To avoid treating financial performance as the only indicator of success.
- **How it works**:
  1. Evaluate economic viability.
  2. Evaluate social impact on people and communities.
  3. Evaluate environmental impact on ecological systems.
  4. Look for trade-offs and whether profit depends on damaging the other two.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this business economically viable?
  - Is it socially just?
  - Is it ecologically defensible?

### Spectrum of Organizations
- **What it is**: A continuum from pure for-profit firms to NGOs, with CSR, hybrids, social enterprises, and B Corps between them.
- **When to use it**: To classify organizational logic and understand different value mixes.
- **How it works**:
  1. Place the organization on the spectrum based on how it balances profit and social/environmental purpose.
  2. Test whether sustainability is peripheral, integrated, or constitutive.
  3. Check whether the business model actually supports the claimed position.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this truly purpose-led or just profit-led with a CSR layer?
  - What kind of value logic is operating here?
  - Are the governance and revenue model consistent with the mission?

### Planetary Boundaries Framework
- **What it is**: A scientific model defining the ecological ceilings humanity should not exceed.
- **When to use it**: To identify which environmental pressures are most materially relevant to an industry or business model.
- **How it works**:
  1. Map business activities against the nine boundaries.
  2. Distinguish issues that are visible from those that are scientifically most critical.
  3. Prioritize action where the business pushes already-transgressed boundaries further.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Which ecological limits does this business stress?
  - Are we addressing the most important issue or just the most visible one?
  - Where is the system already in overshoot?

### Doughnut Economics
- **What it is**: A model with a social foundation inside and an ecological ceiling outside, defining a safe and just operating space in between.
- **When to use it**: When a strategy must balance social inclusion with ecological restraint.
- **How it works**:
  1. Check whether the model helps meet human needs such as health, income, education, equity, and inclusion.
  2. Check whether it breaches ecological ceilings such as climate, biodiversity, water, or pollution.
  3. Judge the business by whether it moves activity into the safe space between both rings.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Does this model improve human wellbeing without overshooting ecological limits?
  - Are we solving one problem by worsening another?
  - Are social and environmental goals being held together?

### Climate Risk Typology
- **What it is**: A set of lenses for understanding climate as both an impact businesses cause and a risk businesses suffer.
- **When to use it**: In climate strategy, resilience planning, and sector risk assessment.
- **How it works**:
  1. Use the inside-out lens for mitigation: how the business affects climate.
  2. Use the outside-in lens for adaptation: how climate affects the business.
  3. Separate transitional risks from physical risks.
  4. Within physical risks, distinguish acute events from chronic shifts.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Are we managing mitigation and adaptation, or just one?
  - Which risks come from policy and market shifts?
  - Which risks come from floods, heat, drought, fire, or long-term warming?

### Sustainability Journey Model
- **What it is**: A progression from reducing harm to restorative impact to regenerative transformation.
- **When to use it**: To assess how ambitious a sustainability strategy really is.
- **How it works**:
  1. Stage 1 - reduce negative impact through efficiency and compliance.
  2. Stage 2 - move toward restorative and positive impact.
  3. Stage 3 - redesign the business to regenerate ecosystems and social systems.
  4. Judge whether the company is doing "less bad" or redesigning the system.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this incremental improvement or genuine transformation?
  - Are we restoring damage or regenerating systems?
  - What stage is this company actually in?

### Decarbonization Pathway
- **What it is**: A business pathway focused on reducing greenhouse gas emissions across the value chain.
- **When to use it**: In climate strategy, operations redesign, sector decarbonization, and capital allocation.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify where emissions sit across the value chain.
  2. Reduce emissions through energy transition, efficiency, redesign, and lower-carbon materials.
  3. Use nature-based or technical removals only for residual emissions.
  4. Treat decarbonization as a strategic redesign problem, not just a reporting exercise.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Where are the biggest emissions sources?
  - What must change operationally to reduce them?
  - Are offsets being misused as a substitute for real change?

### Net Zero Hierarchy and Carbon Offsetting Logic
- **What it is**: The principle that net zero means reducing first and offsetting only what cannot yet be eliminated.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating carbon claims, climate targets, and offsetting strategies.
- **How it works**:
  1. Cut direct emissions first.
  2. Decarbonize energy and operations next.
  3. Work upstream and downstream across the value chain.
  4. Offset only residual emissions that remain hard to eliminate.
  5. Treat offsetting as supplementary, not primary.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this net zero plan credible?
  - Are offsets a bridge or a license to pollute?
  - Does the plan reduce real emissions at source?

### Scope 1, 2, 3 Emissions Framework
- **What it is**: A classification of direct, energy-related indirect, and value-chain emissions.
- **When to use it**: To identify where a company's carbon footprint really sits.
- **How it works**:
  1. Scope 1 - direct emissions from owned and controlled sources.
  2. Scope 2 - purchased electricity, heat, or energy emissions.
  3. Scope 3 - upstream and downstream value-chain emissions.
  4. Pressure-test whether the company is only acting where control is easiest rather than where impact is greatest.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Where do emissions actually sit?
  - Is the company ignoring the hard part of the footprint?
  - What influence levers exist beyond direct control?

### Extended Producer Responsibility
- **What it is**: A regulatory and managerial model that makes producers responsible for end-of-life impacts and waste management.
- **When to use it**: In product design, packaging, textiles, appliances, vehicles, and circular business models.
- **How it works**:
  1. Assign end-of-life accountability to the producer.
  2. Make recyclability and recovery costs visible to the producer.
  3. Use that cost signal to drive design changes upstream.
  4. Expand responsibility beyond the factory gate into the full lifecycle.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Who should bear end-of-life costs?
  - How do we create incentives for better design?
  - Does the business own the downstream consequences of what it sells?

### Decoupling Lens
- **What it is**: A way to evaluate whether economic growth is becoming less dependent on emissions and material throughput.
- **When to use it**: When testing claims that sustainability and growth can coexist.
- **How it works**:
  1. Compare economic output trends with emissions or resource-use trends.
  2. Distinguish relative improvements from absolute reductions.
  3. Check whether efficiency gains are large enough to offset total growth.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is the business or economy truly decoupling?
  - Are we improving intensity while worsening total impact?
  - Is efficiency solving the problem or masking it?

### Circular Economy Principles
- **What it is**: A model for designing out waste, keeping materials in use, and regenerating natural systems.
- **When to use it**: In product design, operations, business model redesign, and waste strategy.
- **How it works**:
  1. Design out waste and pollution from the start.
  2. Keep products and materials in use through reuse, repair, refurbishment, remanufacture, and resale.
  3. Regenerate natural systems where biological flows are involved.
  4. Treat recycling as a last resort, not the core strategy.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Was waste prevented or just managed later?
  - How many useful lives can this material or product have?
  - Does the model regenerate as well as recirculate?

### Linear vs Recycling vs Circular Model
- **What it is**: A comparative lens for distinguishing take-make-waste logic from true circularity.
- **When to use it**: To avoid calling basic recycling "circular."
- **How it works**:
  1. Linear economy - extract, make, use, discard.
  2. Recycling economy - same model with one loop back.
  3. Circular economy - redesign for multiple uses, repair, remanufacture, and system longevity.
  4. Evaluate how much value is preserved in each model.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this actually circular, or just linear with a recycling tail?
  - How much material value is being destroyed?
  - Where in the loop does the model break?

### Butterfly Diagram
- **What it is**: The circular economy map that distinguishes biological cycles from technical cycles.
- **When to use it**: To decide which circular strategy fits a product or material.
- **How it works**:
  1. Sort materials into biological or technical flows.
  2. For technical flows, prefer maintain, reuse, repair, refurbish, remanufacture before recycle.
  3. For biological flows, return nutrients safely to natural systems.
  4. Choose the tightest loop possible because it preserves the most value.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What is the highest-value circular loop available?
  - Should this material return to industry or nature?
  - Are we collapsing too quickly into low-value recycling?

### Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
- **What it is**: A cradle-to-grave measurement method for environmental impacts across sourcing, production, use, and end of life.
- **When to use it**: To compare design choices, verify impact claims, and avoid greenwashing.
- **How it works**:
  1. Map the product lifecycle.
  2. Quantify environmental impacts across stages.
  3. Identify hotspots where most impact sits.
  4. Use results to guide redesign and communication.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Where is the real environmental burden?
  - Are we moving impacts rather than reducing them?
  - Can we substantiate the sustainability claim?

### Design for Environment (DfE)
- **What it is**: A product-design framework that treats materials, use, and end of life as design decisions.
- **When to use it**: In R&D, product redesign, and innovation reviews.
- **How it works**:
  1. Materials - source lower-impact, traceable, fair, and preferably recycled or renewable inputs.
  2. Use - design for durability, repairability, modularity, and accessibility.
  3. End of life - design for disassembly, reuse, take-back, and practical recyclability.
  4. Apply the framework before launch, when leverage is highest.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What impacts are being locked in at design stage?
  - Will this product last, be repaired, and be recovered?
  - Are upstream and downstream impacts being considered early enough?

### Design for Social Sustainability
- **What it is**: A design lens that adds labor conditions, inclusion, affordability, community impact, and transparency to product development.
- **When to use it**: When a product may be environmentally improved but socially harmful or exclusionary.
- **How it works**:
  1. Assess labor standards across the supply chain.
  2. Check accessibility and inclusive design.
  3. Evaluate affordability and access across income groups.
  4. Check local community effects and transparency mechanisms.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Who is excluded, exploited, or ignored by this design?
  - Is accessibility being treated as core, not optional?
  - Does environmental progress rely on social harm elsewhere?

### Design for Disassembly, Mono-Materiality, and Modularity
- **What it is**: A design toolkit for making products repairable, separable, and recoverable in practice.
- **When to use it**: In products that otherwise become unrecoverable because of glued, welded, or mixed-material construction.
- **How it works**:
  1. Use mechanical connections where possible.
  2. Reduce material variety within components.
  3. Make modules replaceable and upgradable.
  4. Mark materials and enable easy sorting.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Can this product be repaired or disassembled by design?
  - Is recyclability theoretical or practical?
  - Are future recovery costs being designed in or designed out?

### Biomimicry
- **What it is**: Designing products and systems by learning from structures and principles found in nature.
- **When to use it**: When seeking radical reductions in material use or smarter performance trade-offs.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify the core function the design must perform.
  2. Look for natural systems that solve similar structural problems.
  3. Translate the principle into product architecture.
  4. Validate whether the redesign reduces materials, weight, cost, or impact.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Are we overengineering because of industry habit?
  - Can nature show a lighter or smarter structure?
  - What hidden assumptions about performance can be challenged?

### Sustainable Business Model Archetypes
- **What it is**: Recurring model types such as recycled inputs, localized supplies, repair services, leasing, take-back, resale, social enterprise, platform models, and material databases.
- **When to use it**: When redesigning how a business creates, delivers, and captures value.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify the dominant impact problem.
  2. Match it to a business model archetype that changes incentives and flows.
  3. Test whether the archetype creates economic, environmental, and social value.
  4. Reject combinations that are incoherent or contradictory.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What kind of business model change fits this problem?
  - Should we sell products, services, access, repair, or information?
  - Is the model internally coherent?

### Business Model Canvas
- **What it is**: A nine-block model for designing a complete sustainable business system.
- **When to use it**: To turn sustainability ideas into an executable business model.
- **How it works**:
  1. Define value proposition and mission.
  2. Identify customer segments, channels, and relationships.
  3. Define revenue streams and cost structure.
  4. Specify key activities, resources, and partners.
  5. Check whether the full system supports both viability and impact.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - How does this model create and capture value?
  - Which capabilities are core and which sit with partners?
  - Is the model coherent across all blocks?

### Challenge-Resource-Feasibility Stress Test
- **What it is**: A practical methodology from the canvas workshop for pressure-testing assumptions.
- **When to use it**: Before committing to a new sustainable business model.
- **How it works**:
  1. Use challenge questions to expose risks and weak assumptions.
  2. Use capability and resource questions to identify execution gaps.
  3. Use feasibility and impact questions to judge readiness and real effect.
  4. Revise or abandon models that are attractive on paper but impossible to execute.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What could break this model?
  - Do we actually have the capabilities to execute it?
  - Is the expected impact meaningful or cosmetic?

### Four Pathways for Sustainable Business Models
- **What it is**: Four routes to building sustainability into a business: start-up, complete transformation, diversification, and acquisition/partnership.
- **When to use it**: When deciding how an organization should pursue sustainability based on its starting point.
- **How it works**:
  1. Start-up - build the mission and model from scratch.
  2. Complete transformation - redesign the core business.
  3. Diversification - add a new sustainable line beside the legacy model.
  4. Acquisition/partnership - access missing capabilities externally.
  5. Choose the path based on resources, urgency, legacy constraints, and integration risk.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Which transformation route fits our context?
  - How much legacy friction do we face?
  - Are speed, coherence, or risk mitigation the priority?

### Lewin's Unfreeze-Change-Refreeze Model
- **What it is**: A three-step change model for deep organizational transformation.
- **When to use it**: In sustainability transitions that require company-wide behavior change.
- **How it works**:
  1. Unfreeze - create urgency, disrupt complacency, and challenge current assumptions.
  2. Change - implement the new processes, roles, and experiments.
  3. Refreeze - institutionalize the change through routines, structures, incentives, and roles.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - How do we mobilize people for change?
  - How do we move from vision to execution?
  - How do we stop backsliding once the initial enthusiasm fades?

### Partner Incentive Design
- **What it is**: The rule that ecosystem partners only sustain collaboration if they also capture value.
- **When to use it**: In circular, regenerative, or multi-stakeholder business models.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify what each partner must contribute.
  2. Identify what incentive each partner needs: financial, reputational, informational, strategic, or social.
  3. Design the model so partners are better off participating than not.
  4. Reject partnership assumptions built on altruism alone.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Why would this partner cooperate?
  - What value do they capture?
  - Is the ecosystem stable or exploitative?

### Regenerative Business Lens
- **What it is**: A lens that asks whether a business restores and improves living systems rather than merely reducing damage.
- **When to use it**: When evaluating ambitious food, agriculture, land-use, or ecosystem-linked models.
- **How it works**:
  1. Redesign the full offer, not just one input.
  2. Align sourcing, operations, customer experience, and ecosystem relationships.
  3. Treat scarcity, seasonality, and ecosystem health as design constraints.
  4. Judge success by improved system health, not only reduced harm.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this business actually regenerative or just cleaner?
  - Have we changed the system or only one ingredient?
  - Does the full offering align with ecological realities?

### Trade-Off and Tension Management Framework
- **What it is**: A framework for recognizing unavoidable tensions such as learning vs performing, openness vs exclusivity, short vs long term, and social vs environmental goals.
- **When to use it**: When sustainability creates legitimate conflicts between desirable objectives.
- **How it works**:
  1. Name the tension explicitly.
  2. Avoid pretending one pole can simply disappear.
  3. Choose a response mode: structural separation, temporal separation, or synthesis.
  4. Revisit the tension as context changes.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What are the real competing demands?
  - Can we separate, sequence, or integrate them?
  - Which approach fits our size and context?

### Systems Thinking
- **What it is**: A way of understanding problems through interconnections, feedback loops, delays, and root causes rather than isolated events.
- **When to use it**: Whenever a simple fix risks treating symptoms while leaving the system intact.
- **How it works**:
  1. Define the problem clearly.
  2. Identify interacting causes, actors, and incentives.
  3. Trace reinforcing and balancing loops.
  4. Find leverage points where interventions change the system, not just the surface.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Are we solving the right problem?
  - What unintended consequences might appear?
  - Where can a small intervention have large system effects?

### System Mapping
- **What it is**: A tool for visualizing the factors, relationships, and feedback loops behind a problem.
- **When to use it**: In messy sustainability problems with many causes and delayed effects.
- **How it works**:
  1. Write the core problem.
  2. Add drivers, actors, and outcomes around it.
  3. Connect them with arrows showing influence.
  4. Identify feedback loops and potential leverage points.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - What is driving this problem?
  - Which variables reinforce the system?
  - Where could intervention change the trajectory?

### Value Network Mapping
- **What it is**: A map of stakeholders, relationships, exchanges, and power across an ecosystem.
- **When to use it**: When a solution depends on coordination across government, suppliers, communities, NGOs, customers, and partners.
- **How it works**:
  1. Identify the relevant actors.
  2. Map what they exchange: money, information, materials, legitimacy, labor, influence.
  3. Identify who has power, who has dependency, and who bears risk.
  4. Use the network to design interventions, alliances, and governance.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Who must move for this system to change?
  - Where is power concentrated?
  - Which actors are missing from the current conversation?

### Tragedy of the Commons
- **What it is**: The dynamic where individually rational extraction leads to collective resource collapse.
- **When to use it**: In fisheries, climate, biodiversity, water, and any shared-resource business problem.
- **How it works**:
  1. Recognize the shared resource and the incentive to overuse it.
  2. Map how individual gain undermines collective sustainability.
  3. Test whether trust, self-regulation, or formal governance can prevent collapse.
  4. Design coordination or regulation if market incentives alone fail.
- **Key questions it answers**:
  - Is this a commons problem?
  - Why is private optimization destroying shared value?
  - What governance mechanism is required to avoid collapse?

## Mental Models
- Externalities do not disappear just because they sit outside the income statement.
- Relative efficiency can still increase absolute harm if total volume keeps growing.
- Scope 3 is often where the real impact lives, even if it is hardest to control.
- Waste is usually a design failure, not an inevitable output.
- If a partner cannot capture value, the ecosystem will not hold.
- Sustainability is rarely a one-department problem; it is design, procurement, operations, finance, marketing, HR, and governance at once.
- Recycling is not the gold standard; tighter loops like repair and reuse preserve more value.
- "Less bad" is not the same as restorative, and restorative is not the same as regenerative.
- If you optimize one node of the system while damaging the rest, you have not solved the problem.
- Trade-offs are not evidence of failure; they are normal conditions of real transformation.
- Institutional change must outlive charismatic leaders and be embedded into routines.
- Shared resources collapse when incentives reward extraction without accountability.

## Anti-Patterns
- **Shareholder-only blindness**: Treating all non-financial impacts as irrelevant unless regulation forces attention.
- **Offset-first climate strategy**: Buying credits before reducing real emissions.
- **Scope 3 avoidance**: Reporting only the small part of the footprint the company directly controls.
- **Recycling theater**: Claiming circularity while leaving the core take-make-waste model intact.
- **Green product, dirty system**: Improving one product attribute while keeping exploitative sourcing, wasteful logistics, or harmful labor practices.
- **One-team sustainability**: Delegating the whole agenda to CSR or ESG staff while core functions stay unchanged.
- **Partnership romanticism**: Assuming stakeholders will collaborate without clear incentives.
- **Design myopia**: Focusing on launch cost and features while locking in downstream waste and exclusion.
- **Incremental substitution bias**: Swapping one ingredient or material while leaving the business architecture untouched.
- **Tech solutionism**: Assuming AI, blockchain, or traceability tools solve incentive and governance problems by themselves.
- **Compliance minimalism**: Doing only what regulation requires and calling it strategy.
- **Exporting the problem**: Moving waste, pollution, or labor abuse out of sight and calling the business cleaner.
- **Impact without evidence**: Making sustainability claims without LCA, monitoring, third-party verification, or lifecycle logic.
- **Commons exploitation**: Maximizing private extraction from shared resources until the system degrades for everyone.

## How This Discipline Challenges Others
- **Strategy**: It challenges strategy to count externalities, stakeholder harms, and system fragility, not just competitive advantage and profit pools.
- **Entrepreneurship**: It challenges founders who celebrate speed and validation while ignoring who pays for the model's hidden social or ecological costs.
- **Product Management**: It challenges product teams to treat lifecycle, repairability, inclusion, and end-of-life as product requirements, not adjacent concerns.
- **Finance**: It challenges financial analysis that discounts long-term ecological and social risk because it is hard to price today.
- **Operations**: It challenges efficiency programs that optimize local metrics while worsening total material throughput, labor conditions, or emissions.
- **Innovation**: It challenges innovation theater by insisting that novelty is irrelevant if the system-level impact is still destructive.

## Key Vocabulary
- **Shareholder value maximization**: The view that the firm's purpose is to maximize returns to owners.
- **Stakeholder value creation**: The view that firms should create long-term value for all affected stakeholders.
- **Primary stakeholders**: Directly connected groups such as employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and investors.
- **Secondary stakeholders**: Indirect but influential groups such as NGOs, media, associations, and competitors.
- **Triple bottom line**: Performance across people, planet, and profit.
- **Planetary boundaries**: Ecological ceilings beyond which system stability is threatened.
- **Doughnut Economics**: Safe and just operating space between social foundation and ecological ceiling.
- **Transitional risk**: Climate-related risk from regulation, markets, technology, or preferences changing.
- **Physical risk**: Climate-related risk from weather events or long-term climatic change.
- **Acute risk**: Event-based physical risk such as floods, heatwaves, storms, or fires.
- **Chronic risk**: Long-duration physical risk such as temperature rise or water stress.
- **Net zero**: Reduce emissions deeply and offset only residuals that cannot yet be removed.
- **Scope 1, 2, 3**: Direct, energy-related indirect, and value-chain emissions categories.
- **Extended Producer Responsibility**: Producer accountability for end-of-life product impacts.
- **Decoupling**: Separating economic growth from emissions or resource consumption.
- **Circular economy**: Design out waste, keep materials in use, regenerate natural systems.
- **Butterfly diagram**: Circular economy map of biological and technical loops.
- **Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)**: Measurement of impacts across the full product lifecycle.
- **Mono-materiality**: Designing components with one material to ease separation and recycling.
- **Modularity**: Designing products with replaceable components.
- **Repairability**: Ease with which a product can be fixed and kept in use.
- **Remanufacturing**: Restoring used products or components to functional condition for resale or reuse.
- **Regeneration**: Improving and restoring living systems rather than only reducing harm.
- **Business Model Canvas**: Nine-block model for value creation, delivery, and capture.
- **Value network**: The ecosystem of actors exchanging resources, influence, and value.
- **Systems thinking**: Problem solving through interdependence, feedback loops, and root causes.
- **Tragedy of the Commons**: Shared-resource collapse driven by individually rational overuse.

## Decision Criteria
**STRONG signal:**
- The model creates value for customers without offloading hidden social or environmental costs onto weaker stakeholders.
- The company can explain its impact across the full lifecycle, including sourcing, use, and end of life.
- Scope 3 and ecosystem effects are acknowledged, not ignored.
- Partners, communities, and suppliers have clear incentives and fair value capture.
- The model uses tighter circular loops such as repair, reuse, refurbishment, or remanufacturing before defaulting to recycling.
- Environmental and social claims are backed by evidence, metrics, and verification.
- Trade-offs are named explicitly and managed with a credible strategy rather than denied.
- The business model remains viable under stakeholder scrutiny, regulation, and long-term ecological constraints.

**WEAK signal:**
- Sustainability appears as a feature layer or marketing message rather than a redesign of incentives and flows.
- The company focuses on direct operations while most impact sits upstream or downstream.
- The model depends on customers or partners behaving altruistically without incentive design.
- The company improves efficiency per unit but ignores total growth in throughput.
- Impact logic exists, but measurement, governance, or partner alignment is still immature.
- Social and environmental goals are both mentioned, but one is clearly being subordinated in practice.

**RED FLAG:**
- Profitability depends on dumping waste, emissions, labor abuse, or risk onto parties with less power.
- Offsetting or ESG language is being used to avoid operational change.
- The model is called circular, but products are still hard to repair, disassemble, or recover.
- The firm cannot say who bears the costs across the value chain.
- Stakeholders are referenced rhetorically but excluded from actual decision-making.
- The business exploits a commons and has no credible governance or restraint mechanism.
- The company treats regulation as the only reason to improve, with no internal strategic logic for responsibility.
- Growth assumptions require ecological overshoot or social harm to remain attractive.
